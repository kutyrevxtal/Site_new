Melt-rock interaction

Many of the rocks I work on cannot be understood as closed systems. Their textures, mineral assemblages, and chemical patterns show that melts and later fluids interacted with pre-existing ultramafic rocks, modified local equilibrium, and generated new minerals and ore-related features. Melt-rock interaction is therefore not a secondary complication in my work. It is often the process that explains why a rock looks the way it does.

I approach this problem through petrography, mineral chemistry, inclusion studies, and geochemical modelling. In chromitite-related systems, some of the clearest evidence comes from multiphase inclusions in PGE alloys. These inclusions contain mineral combinations that are difficult to reconcile with crystallization from a single primitive melt and instead record interaction with evolving melts, aqueous fluids, or metamorphic reaction media. Similar reasoning applies more broadly to ultramafic rocks that preserve overprints from serpentinization, metasomatism, and later recrystallization. I am interested in identifying which features are inherited from the magmatic stage and which formed during reaction. That distinction matters for both petrogenesis and mineral exploration, because ore minerals and their hosts may record several chemically distinct events. My goal is to reconstruct those events rather than forcing the rock into a single-stage model.
